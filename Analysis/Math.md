# Chladni Figures on a Rectangular Plate

Chladni figures arise from solutions of the wave equation, which describes how vibrations propagate through a solid surface.

The general wave equation is a partial differential equation (PDE), and solving it in its most general form can be complex. However, when the vibrating surface is a rectangular plate, the problem can be simplified by applying boundary conditions that describe how the plate is fixed or supported.

Under these conditions, the vibration modes of the plate can be approximated using standing wave solutions that depend on the plate dimensions.

The allowed vibration modes are related to wavelengths that "fit" the plate geometry:

$$u(x,y,t) = sin(n\pi x/l) sin(m\pi y/h)$$

*n* and *m* are the mode of oscillation. 

It's defined by the frequency, the material, and some other parameters. 

After some research, the solution of the PDE is not that simple.  
The reality of the methodology of displaying the Chadlini figure is to take defined n and m, like (1,2) or (2,3), and use them to compute the nodes.
It's difficult to define the frequency at which the patterns appear.
